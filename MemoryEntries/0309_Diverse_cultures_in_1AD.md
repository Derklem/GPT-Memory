How is Zoroastrianism different from the Abrahamic religions?

**assistant:**

Zoroastrianism, founded by the prophet Zoroaster (or Zarathustra), predates the Abrahamic religions and originated in ancient Persia (modern-day Iran). While Zoroastrianism is monotheistic, with the worship of Ahura Mazda as the supreme deity, it differs from the Abrahamic religions in several ways:

1. **Dualism:** Zoroastrianism incorporates dualistic elements, emphasizing the struggle between good and evil, represented by Ahura Mazda (the god of light and goodness) and Angra Mainyu (the destructive spirit of darkness).

2. **Cosmology:** Zoroastrianism has a distinct cosmology that includes the belief in a cosmic battle between the forces of good and evil, with an ultimate triumph of good in the end times.

3. **Scripture:** The primary religious texts of Zoroastrianism are the Avesta and the Gathas, attributed to Zoroaster himself. These texts contain hymns, prayers, and teachings about ethics and rituals.

4. **Afterlife:** Zoroastrianism teaches about the judgment of souls after death, where individuals are judged based on their deeds in life and rewarded or punished accordingly in the afterlife.

5. **Influence:** While the Abrahamic religions have had a significant impact on world history and culture, Zoroastrianism has been less influential in terms of global spread and number of adherents, although it has had a profound influence on the development of ethical monotheism.

Despite these differences, there are also similarities, such as the emphasis on ethical behavior, belief in a single supreme deity, and the concept of a final judgment.
