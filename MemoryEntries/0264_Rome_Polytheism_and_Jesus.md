The Romans were generally pragmatic in their approach to religion, often allowing conquered peoples to continue practicing their own religions as long as these practices did not threaten Roman authority or social order. They would sometimes even incorporate foreign deities into their own pantheon, a practice known as "interpretatio Romana."

Crucifixion was not specifically reserved for those who opposed the Roman pantheon of gods. Instead, it was primarily used as a method of execution for slaves, rebels, and those considered enemies of the state. It was a particularly brutal and public form of punishment intended to serve as a deterrent to others.

In the case of Jesus, his crucifixion was more about political concerns than religious ones. He was seen as a potential threat to Roman order because he was perceived to be a claimant to the title of "King of the Jews," which could incite rebellion against Roman rule. The Roman authorities, particularly Pontius Pilate, saw his execution as a way to prevent any potential uprising.

Thus, while the Romans were generally tolerant of different religious practices within their empire, they were ruthless in suppressing any political movements that threatened their control.
